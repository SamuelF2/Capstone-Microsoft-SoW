# Deal Approval in MCEM for ISD: Roles, Responsibilities & Criteria

**Microsoft's Customer Engagement Methodology (MCEM)** for Industry Solutions Delivery (ISD) includes a critical **Deal Approval** step before a consulting deal is finalized with the customer. After the solution has been crafted and priced ("Prepare Solution"), an internal **Deal Review Meeting (DRM)** is held with key approvers to **validate the proposal's technical solution, delivery plan, pricing, and terms**.

## Overview

The goal is to ensure the deal is sound – meeting customer needs while adhering to Microsoft's risk and financial guidelines – **before granting internal approval to proceed** to customer negotiation. This process involves multiple personas, each with distinct roles, responsibilities, and decision criteria. Recent FY25/FY26 updates to MCEM have refined this approval framework (introducing a new empowerment model with four approval levels) to streamline decisions and focus approvers on the right deals.

This document breaks down the **key approver personas** in the deal approval process, including:

- Sales Managers (Area Consulting Leads)
- Pursuit Leads
- Delivery Managers (Area Delivery Leaders)
- Consulting Practice Leads (CPLs)
- Client Delivery Partners (CDPs)
- Regional Delivery Leaders (RDL) & Senior Executives

Each section details **their role in the DRM, responsibilities, decision criteria, and the rubrics/thresholds** they use when evaluating a deal for approval.

## Additional Insights and Process Notes

### 1. Deal Review Meeting (DRM) Structure

The DRM is a formal checkpoint run by the Pursuit Lead and Delivery Lead. This cross-functional team reviews the deal from all angles (technology, delivery, sales, finance).

**Key Participants:**

- **ESAP approver(s)** for the deal (CPL, ADL, or RDL depending on level)
- **Area Consulting Lead** (representing sales management)
- **ISD Seller** (Account Executive or Specialist)
- **Client Delivery Partner** (if assigned to the account)
- **Lead Solution Architect(s)** who designed the solution
- **Consulting Project Manager (CPM)** who contributed to planning
- **Quality Assurance Director (QAD)** or Risk Manager

**Outcome:** The DRM typically results in an approval to proceed or a "no-go" pending specific changes. If approved, the Pursuit Lead uploads final documentation and initiates the formal approval workflow in CompassOne, where the designated approver records the decision (and can add any approval conditions in the system).

### 2. Quality and Risk Reviews

Prior to and during deal approval, **independent reviews** feed into the decision.

**Large Deals (Types 0/1):**

- A **Risk Management & Quality Assurance (RMQA) lead** conducts a Deal Quality review in parallel
- Produces a report on risks, mitigations, and lessons learned applied
- Report is considered by the approvers

**Smaller Deals (Type 2/3):**

- A formal RMQA review may not be mandatory
- The **delivery leadership itself and perhaps a peer QA reviewer** in the area perform the risk check during the DRM

**Technical Reviews (Required for All Deals):**

- The **Enterprise Architecture Group (EAG)** must review especially complex solutions
- Automatically engaged for Type 0/1 deals
- Regional/domain architects handle technical review for lower tiers
- Reviews assess whether the solution design is sound and leverages the right expertise

**Approval Process:** Approvers will generally *not* green-light a deal until QA and technical reviewers have confirmed quality standards are met (or their concerns have been resolved). In the CompassOne workflow, approvers see confirmations that required checks (financial model validation, GDPR check, etc.) are completed before they hit "Approve."

### 3. Rubrics and Checklists

Microsoft uses the **Microsoft Professional Services Policy (MPSP)** and associated business rules as the foundation for deal approval. These policies enumerate specific requirements (e.g., minimum margins, when legal must review terms, anti-corruption checks for certain geographies, etc.).

Many of these rules are built into the tooling – for example, CompassOne will flag if a deal has non-standard terms and require a Deal Desk consultation, or if a project plan doesn't have a mandatory component.

**Common Review Rubric:**

1. **Deal Fundamentals:**
   - Is the **scope** clear and achievable?
   - Are customer **outcomes** clearly defined?
   - Is there a defined **work breakdown and schedule**?
   - Are we within allowed **discounts and margins**?

2. **Solution Specifics:**
   - Have we accounted for technical complexities?
   - Did we reuse relevant IP/lessons?
   - Is the architecture vetted by experts (EAG or others)?

3. **Special Scenarios:**
   - Any special circumstances (e.g., working with an unvetted partner, handling sensitive data, government customer requirements)?
   - Have the appropriate experts (legal, compliance, special boards) cleared it?

4. **Compliance & Controls:**
   - All anti-corruption, privacy, and regulatory checks passed?
   - If deal involves personal data handling, has an infosec review been done?
   - If it's in a regulated industry, are there additional governance steps?

5. **Customer & Delivery Readiness:**
   - Is the client ready to proceed (approved budget, signed off on scope)?
   - Is the delivery team in place or a solid staffing plan ready (including cross-group collaboration if needed)?

Each persona views these through their lens – e.g., a Sales leader prioritizes business fit and client commitment, whereas a Delivery leader drills into resourcing and risk mitigations – but these elements form a **shared framework** ensuring nothing falls through the cracks.

### 4. RACI and Support Roles

The MCEM RACI (Responsible-Accountable-Consulted-Informed) matrix for Inspire & Design stages provides detailed guidance on who does what in pursuits:

- **Pursuit Lead:** Responsible for assembling the solution and getting all reviews done
- **ADL:** Accountable for the "Deal Approval" activity
- **Architects and others:** Consulted
- **Various managers:** Informed

**Supporting Roles:**

While approvers make the final call, they heavily rely on inputs from:

- **Solution Architect** (typically *Consulted*) – ensures the technical integrity
- **Consulting Project Manager** – checks project structure and scheduling
- **Resource Manager** – for staffing feasibility
- **Deal Desk/Contracts manager** – for any non-standard terms

**Example:** Before a DRM, the seller or PL must engage the Deal Desk via a DD360 request if there are any contract deviations, so that a contracts expert's recommendations are available for the approvers. In the federal sector, they even require a Federal Contracts Manager and legal to review solicitations pre-DRM.

### 5. FY25/FY26 Process Updates

The transition from the old Lead-to-Order process to MCEM for ISD brought fewer approval gates but more **empowered front-line decision-making**.

**Key Changes:**

- **Type 0 Level Introduction:** New level for extremely large deals requiring Corporate VP sign-off (effective Feb 2025)
- **Threshold Adjustments:** Values reflect Azure consumption emphasis
- **Reduced Escalations:** E.g., a $1M services deal can now be approved within the practice or account team (CPL/CDP) quickly
- **Maintained Rigor:** On big/high-risk deals

**Training and Updates:**

Approvers at all levels have been given updated training (deal coaching sessions, battle cards) on these changes.

**FY26 Focus Areas:**

- **Responsible AI and new solution risks** – deals involving new AI technologies or scenarios may invoke additional review steps (e.g., an AI ethics review) as part of the approval criteria, per updated business rules
- Approvers must stay current with evolving guidelines
- Reference the latest **Empowerment Model battle card** and **Special Conditions list** during their review
- Deals above a certain partner subcontractor percentage or in certain countries might automatically elevate to the next approver level

## Summary

**Deal approval in MCEM for ISD is a multi-perspective governance process**. Each persona – from Sales Manager to Pursuit Lead to Delivery and Practice Leads – has a clear role in vetting the deal against their criteria. The **combination of their approvals** ensures that by the time we get the internal "green light" to proceed to contract, the deal is profitable, deliverable, compliant, and aligned with customer success.

This rigorous but streamlined approval process (especially with the recent empowerment model updates) helps Microsoft **balance speed and control**: empowering teams to close most deals quickly at lower levels, while applying executive scrutiny to the largest or riskiest engagements. The result is a disciplined approach to closing deals that protects both **customer outcomes and Microsoft's business**. Each approver persona plays a crucial part in achieving that balance.
