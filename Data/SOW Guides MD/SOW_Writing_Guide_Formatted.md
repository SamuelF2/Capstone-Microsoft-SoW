## Getting Started

Welcome to the Statement of Work Writing Guide!

This document has been created to help you write a great Statement of Work (SoW)!

In this document, you will find best practices, helpful tips and some examples that your colleagues have written for their customers. We can continue to grow and evolve this document with your support - so please feel free to share your examples, experiences, and feedback.

Each section of the SoW communicates part of the story about the project, building on the previous sections.

### How to Use This Document

This document is intended as an aid to help you get the information you need when you need it. It is organized by the sections of the SoW template to help you zero in on the specific areas you need help.

The best way to utilize this document for the first time is to start with the introductory sections including:
- The SoW in context
- Language to avoid

Once you have the introductory content in mind, jump to the specific section of the SoW where you need help. For example, if you want some guidance and examples to help you write a good scope description, refer to the Project objectives and scope section of this document.

Each time you need help with writing your SoW, refer to this document for the section-by-section guidance you need.

### The SoW in Context

The SoW is always attached to an Enterprise Services Work Order (WO). The WO contains fee information, the term and includes a reference to the customers Master Agreement. When the WO is signed by the customer and Microsoft, the WO and the SoW associated with it form our delivery baseline and legal basis for performing services. The SoW is part of the contract - so we need to get it right!

Our fees and standard contractual terms e.g. warranties, IP ownership, data protection, liabilities etc. are included in the WO. If your customer wishes to alter any of these, work with your local Contract Executive or Commercial Manager.

For more information on how the SoW fits as part of the Services contracting hierarchy, check out the guidance on K360.

### How Do You Structure a Program Statement of Work?

As the complexity of our delivery increases and customers demand solutions that involve multiple Domains or catalog packages (formerly offers), our contracts are becoming more complex. We are no longer writing just one statement of work (SoW) for a single solution, but we are having to combine multiple delivery components and various SoWs in one contract to develop solutions for our customers.

There is a need for a standard approach to creating statements of work that support multiple projects and work streams (a program). You will find guidance in the Appendix that provides direction on how to structure your SoW for such situations leveraging the standard templates available in K360 along with any custom SoWs.

### Language to Avoid

There are certain words and phrases you should avoid during the creation of your SoW:

#### Best Efforts
What does this mean? Does this mean that our "best" effort is around-the-clock response and/or work? The term "best efforts" is just too hard to quantify and should be left out of the contract.
- **Alternative**: work with you, commercially reasonable efforts, we will work with you in good faith, collaborate with you

#### Ensure
This implies 100-percent certainty, and we cannot give that.
- **Alternative**: validate, facilitate, coordinate

#### Experts
This word typically implies some sort of additional qualification and/or certification. It can create an argument that we have warranted that these people are special.
- **Alternative**: knowledgeable or experienced individual, subject matter specialist

#### Highest/Best Quality (or Standard)
This is the highest possible standard for our deliverable, but poses a potential warranty issue.
- **Alternative**: commercially reasonable efforts

#### Sales Language
Avoid sales language and do not promise results for deliverables.

#### Service Level Agreements (SLA)
Avoid whenever possible! Be very cautious here. If you agree, you need to baseline the existing level to verify it is accurate. For these scenarios work with your local Contract Executive, Commercial Manager and/or SQA Lead.

#### Specific Delivery Dates
Don't promise any start, milestone, or end date. These can change depending on when the contract is signed.
- **Alternative**: Estimated timeframes/schedules

#### Time is of the Essence
This is an actual legal phrase that means both parties understand that time is of critical importance.
- **Alternative**: Estimated timeframes/schedules

#### Warrant and Represent
These words create an express warranty or are part of a statement that the customer is entitled to rely upon.
- **Alternative**: Confirm, advise, assist, support

#### Will be Free of Defects
This phrasing creates a warranty that what we deliver will be, essentially, perfect.

#### Will Meet and/or Exceed the Customer's Needs
These phrases are pretty vague and arguably create a warranty.
- **Alternative**: work toward your goals/requirements/expectations

#### Best Practice
This is a bit vague. Generally, the practices we are recommending are what Microsoft considers best for our products.
- **Alternative**: Microsoft recommended practice

## Title Page

### Statement of Work Title Page

**Why do we have this section?**
This is the cover page for the document, it includes details on the project name and document details.

**Things to think about:**
Make sure that you reference the SoW by the subject, date and version in the final SoW document when attaching to your work order.

**Completing this section:**
There are four elements to be updated, replace all pink text with 'Normal' style:
- **Subject** – the name of the project
- **Customer name** – customer organization
- **Author(s)** – Microsoft document authors
- **Date** – the date of the document (updated automatically)
- **Version** – the version of the document, issue initial drafts as minor version numbers e.g. V0.1, V0.2 and switch to V1.0 for issue as final.

**FAQ:**
- Q: Can we add the names of the MS contributors?
- A: Yes, feel free to add additional authors and contributors as appropriate for your project.

## Pre-introduction

### Pre-introduction Text

**Why do we have this section?**
The SoW is only one part of the contract, the other element is the Work Order. In this section, we reference the WO and explain that the SoW is part of the contract stack. We also include a statement that the SoW and WO will expire 30 days after their publication date.

**Things to think about:**
Locate the Work Order and locate the WO number.

**Completing this section:**
There are three elements to be updated:
- **Work order number** – obtained from ESWO
- **Customer name** – customer organization
- **Project** – a title for the project that will resonate for the customer

**⚠️ Be careful:**
- If extending the expiration period for the SoW and associated Work Order, be careful if rates are likely to change.

## Introduction

**Why do we have this section?**
In this section, we provide the customer with the context on what this project is and how it relates to their objectives. Think of this section as the executive summary or the Customer "Why".

**Things to think about:**
This section should be succinct, targeting your customer sponsor. Introductions are a good place to describe any events or actions that preceded our involvement.

**⚠️ Be careful:**
- Avoid mentioning benefits or value statements, such as cost reduction or ROI.
- Avoid making broad statements about the customer's overall objectives.

**Example:**
> Contoso is enabling new ways of working, focused on the needs of end-users and providing the communication and collaboration tools needed to improve productivity and efficiency. This Modern Workplace initiative is being delivered by Partner A. As part of that initiative, Partner A has approached Microsoft to assist. This SoW details the services Microsoft will provide Partner A to assist in meeting Contoso's objectives for the Modern Workplace initiative.

## Project Objectives and Scope

### 1. Objectives

**Why do we have this section?**
We highlight what the objectives are for the work that we will deliver. This could be considered the Microsoft "Why".

**Things to think about:**
Well written, measurable objectives are items that we can 'tick off' at the end of the project.

**⚠️ Be careful:**
- Keep the context to the Microsoft project
- Avoid references to things beyond our control
- Avoid Business Result statements

**Examples:**
1. The project objective is to implement a solution for mobile sales and multichannel customer care based on Microsoft Dynamics CRM.
2. The objective of this project is to design and build a prototype of the <insert application name> application.

### 2. Areas in Scope

#### 2.1 General Project Scope

**Why do we have this section?**
We are drawing the boundaries around the work that will be performed. This is where we describe WHAT we will deliver.

**Things to think about:**
- **Quantify, Quantify, Quantify**: Number of interfaces, reports, people trained, customer locations involved, etc.
- Use clear and concise language
- Can we phase the delivery?

**Completing this section:**
When populating the table:
- **Area** – Structure by service, feature, function, or work stream
- **Description** – A short description of the Area
- **Assumptions** – Applicable assumptions for that Area

#### 2.2 Software Products and Technologies

**Why do we have this section?**
We call out the software products and technologies needed to deliver the project.

**Completing this section:**
- **Product and technology item**
- **Version**
- **Responsibility** – which party is responsible for acquiring the item(s)
- **Ready by** – Express timing as "2 weeks after Plan phase" or "project week 10"

#### 2.3 Data Migration

**Why do we have this section?**
Data migration can be complicated and time intensive.

**Completing this section:**
- **Data source** – where the data will be migrated from
- **Data volume** – volume of data in appropriate units
- **Migration mechanism** – tool-based migration, manual, etc.

#### 2.4 System Integration

**Why do we have this section?**
System integration is where two or more systems or components need to connect.

**Completing this section:**
- **Integration** – name of the integration in scope
- **Description of scope** – specific work we will deliver
- **Responsibility** – who is responsible
- **Ready by** – when the integration work needs completion

#### 2.5 Environments

**Why do we have this section?**
This section identifies any environments required to deliver your project.

**Completing this section:**
- **Environment** – name of the environment
- **Location** – customer data center, Microsoft lab, cloud, etc.
- **Responsibility** – who is responsible
- **Ready by** – when you need the environment operational

#### 2.6 Testing and Defect Remediation

**Why do we have this section?**
Most projects will include some elements of testing.

**Things to think about:**
Time boxing the Microsoft effort for customer testing we support is critical.

**⚠️ Be careful:**
- Testing is often underestimated
- Call out testing assumptions
- Test data will be provided by Customer

#### 2.7 Training

**Why do we have this section?**
This section captures any formal training we may be delivering.

**Completing this section:**
- **Type** – what is the type of training
- **Description (including location)**
- **Quantity and duration** – number of sessions, duration, number of participants

### 3. Areas Out of Scope

**Why do we have this section?**
Although we state "any area not explicitly included in 'Areas in scope' is out of scope", it is important to call out some general areas.

**⚠️ Be careful:**
- Avoid duplication
- Less is more
- Specify items that might normally be considered in scope

## Appendices

### Appendix 1 – Executive Summary Writing Guidance

#### What is an Executive Summary?

An executive summary is different from an abstract or introduction.

- **Abstract**: Overview and orientation in 100 to 500 words
- **Executive Summary**: A summary of the document
- **Introduction**: Background information on the subject matter

#### Elements of an Executive Summary

An executive summary needs to clearly define:
1. The problem
2. The solution
3. Key considerations

#### The Style of an Executive Summary in English

Guidelines:
- Use definite, specific, concrete language
- The first sentence introduces the topic
- Keep connected words in a sentence closely together
- Make it coherent
- Avoid technical jargon
- Use the present tense
- Use the active voice
- Put statements in positive form
- Express coordinate ideas in similar form
- Be concise
- Be coherent
- Cover one idea per paragraph

#### Steps to Prepare a Quality Professional Executive Summary

1. Complete the document
2. Define the key points and the main message outline
3. Develop the structural outline draft
4. Write the first draft in one sitting
5. Revise the draft (Is it clear, concise, coherent?)
6. Ask somebody for review and feedback
7. Revise the text again
8. Correct grammar, spelling, punctuation and layout

### Appendix 2 – Building a Statement of Work for a Program

#### Multiple SoW Templates for Programs

There are two SoW templates available when you need to contract for a program:
1. **Program SoW** (for simple programs with 3+ projects)
2. **Complex Program Leadership SoW** (for complex programs with 4+ projects requiring PMO)

| Program SoW | Complex Program Leadership SoW |
|-------------|--------------------------------|
| Simple program with three (3) or more projects | Complex programs with four (4) or more projects |
| Full PMO not needed | PMO is needed |
| Complex program teams NOT involved | Complex program teams are leading |
| May be multiple domains, one driving | Multiple domains, each with significant work |

#### Guidance for Using the Program SoW Template

**When does this guidance apply?**
This guidance helps you contract when combining multiple projects that are part of a larger program.

**Things to think about:**
Work with your SQA Lead and/or Contract Executive/Commercial Manager to evaluate the best SoW approach.

**There are two basic options:**
1. **Separate documents**: Program SoW + separate project Exhibits
2. **Single SoW document**: All content in one document

**⚠️ Be careful:**
- There should be a single owner of the Program SoW
- Consolidate all common content into a single location
- Check language for consistency
- Different fee structures require clear delineation
- Build overall staffing carefully
- Ensure out of scope items don't conflict

**Steps to follow:**
1. Identify all applicable standard SoW templates
2. Use latest global SoW template for custom services
3. Create a Program SoW using the template
4. Create project Exhibits from each SoW template
5. Reference the Program SoW and project Exhibits in the ESWO
6. Upload final documents to CompassOne

### Appendix 3 – SoW Project Approach Library

#### Approach - Microsoft Online Solution Lifecycle

The project will be structured following the Microsoft Online Solution Lifecycle delivery methodology across three distinct phases:

1. **Remediate**
2. **Enable**
3. **Migrate**

Each phase has distinct activities and deliverables.

##### Engagement Initiation

**Microsoft activities:**
- Engage with Customer team to identify key stakeholders
- Review scope including in scope, assumptions, out of scope, and timelines
- Establish proper communication
- Validate SharePoint Portfolio Assessment has been conducted

**Customer activities:**
- Attend and participate in preinitiation call
- Assign project initiation responsibilities
- Complete project initiation prerequisites
- Staff the project with required resources

##### General Project Activities

**Microsoft activities:**
- Prepare for and conduct project kickoff meeting
- Document conditions of satisfaction and critical success factors
- Create a RAID log (Risk, Actions, Issues, Decisions)
- Generate a project communication matrix
- Create preliminary project status report
- Produce a project plan for Microsoft activities

**Remediate Phase:**
- Generate weekly project status reports
- Provide technical guidance and assistance
- Provide input on user communications

**Enable Phase:**
- Produce test cases
- Complete Enable phase activities
- Complete in-scope testing
- Produce delivery summary documentation

**Migrate Phase:**
- Complete Migrate phase activities for in-scope components

**Customer activities:**
- Provide project manager resources
- Manage scheduling and logistics
- Provide project resources and SMEs
- Make timely design and planning decisions
- Review RAID log
- Develop project communication matrix
- Produce and manage project plan for Customer activities

**General Project Component Deliverables:**

| Name | Description | Phase | Acceptance Required? | Responsibility |
|------|-------------|-------|---------------------|----------------|
| Project Plan | Key Microsoft activities, milestones, dependencies, and durations | All | Yes | Microsoft |
| Preparation Checklist | Tasks and resources required by Customer | Assess | No | Microsoft |
| Design and Plan | Design decisions and high-level plan | Enable | Yes | Microsoft |
| Delivery Summary | Work completed summary and next steps | Completion | No | Microsoft |

##### Project Components and Deliverables

**Remediation Checkpoints (REM-01)**

**Microsoft activities:**
- Facilitate checkpoints with Customer to review progress
- Perform project management to monitor remediation activities
- Guide Customer remediation activities
- Verify necessary remediation has been completed

**Customer activities:**
- Perform required steps to complete remediation activities
- Lead remediation activities
- Provide status of remediation progress

**Key assumptions:**
- Customer has resources with knowledgeable skill set
- Remediation phase will not last longer than 8 weeks

**Remediation Assistance (REM-02)**

**Microsoft activities:**
- Facilitate checkpoints with Customer
- Perform project management to monitor activities
- Guide Customer remediation activities
- Verify necessary remediation completed
- Assist Customer resources with remediation items

**Customer activities:**
- Perform required steps to complete remediation
- Lead remediation activities through oversight
- Provide status of remediation progress

**Key assumptions:**
- Customer has resources with knowledgeable skill set
- Remediation phase will not last longer than 8 weeks
